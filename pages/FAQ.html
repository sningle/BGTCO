“Coming out” is the process of telling other people about your sexuality or gender identity. Despite how coming out is sometimes portrayed in popular media, it’s often an ongoing process and is different for everyone. Some people may choose to come out to everyone in their life and talk openly about their identity. Others may choose to only share information about their sexuality or gender identity with family, friends, or partners. Ultimately, coming out is a process that is unique to you and can take many different forms depending on what you’re comfortable with.

Coming out is a deeply personal choice that shouldn’t be taken lightly. You shouldn’t feel pressured to come out if you’re not ready or simply don’t wish to do so. Ultimately, coming out is just a way of letting people know about your gender identity or sexuality. Some may choose to come out because they want people to know they go by different pronouns or a different name than they did previously. Others may simply want to celebrate their identity with the people around them or normalize their identity in their everyday life. Whatever the reason, coming out should be a choice you make because you want to, not because you feel you “should” or even “have to.”

There is no “right” or “wrong” way to label yourself, as the way you label (or choose not to label) your sexuality or gender is entirely up to you. Many people fear they will eventually “change their mind” or have to come out again if they’re “wrong,” but, while it’s possible you will later choose to label yourself differently, there’s nothing wrong with selecting a label that you feel fits you, even if it may be temporary. If you eventually choose to identify differently, there’s also no shame in coming out however many times you need to.

Not everyone wants to put a label on their gender or sexuality and that’s okay. If you choose not to label yourself but still want to come out, there are lots of ways to do it. Some people may choose to say they’re queer or not straight / not cis without elaborating further. Others may choose to simply state that they don’t resonate with their assigned gender at birth or don’t feel they’re only attracted to members of the opposite sex. It’s also an option to say you’re still questioning or that you’re exploring your identity if you feel you’ll eventually settle on a label but don’t currently have one. However you choose to describe your identity as well as whether or not you choose to label it is up to you and should make you comfortable, even if others don’t necessarily ‘get it’ initially.

Your decision to come out is completely up to you, thus you can come out whenever you want. There’s no one way to be “sure” about your identity, although some may choose to experiment with a label, name, or set of pronouns before sharing them with others. Some people may also choose to share these things with one or two people at first to see if it feels comfortable before telling others. Whatever the case, you should come out when it feels right to you.

Often the best way to tell others your pronouns is to simply tell them! If the person is a friend or family member, you might send a text, call them, or meet them in person to let them know you’d like them to begin using a different set of pronouns for you. If you have a less personal relationship with the person, such as with a teacher or coworker, you might send an email indicating your new pronouns instead. Other options for letting people know your pronouns are adding them to your email signature, changing your display name to include your pronouns, or introducing yourself by stating your pronouns after your name. There are lots of ways to let people know your pronouns, so do whatever feels right for you!

It’s not uncommon for people who know a person’s pronouns to occasionally misgender them. If it’s a rare occurrence, it’s likely that the person is simply adjusting to using new pronouns for you. If this becomes a common occurrence, however, it can be disheartening. If the person is a friend or family member, giving a gentle verbal reminder or sending them a text as a reminder may be helpful. If the person is a coworker or teacher, you might send them an email to remind them of your pronouns.

Alternatively, if someone is intentionally misgendering you, there are steps you can take to avoid this in the future. If this person is a family member or friend, let them know that using your correct pronouns are not simply “preferred” but required for having a relationship with you. Though they may not fully understand or relate to your identity, you should let them know that you still deserve the respect of having your pronouns honored. Although it may be difficult, set a firm boundary and stick to it. If they continue to intentionally misgender you, you may want to reconsider your relationship with this person. Ultimately, regardless of whether others respect your pronouns, you and your identity are valid and deserve respect.

If the person is a coworker, teacher, medical professional, or any other person in a professional setting, there are other options for handling being intentionally misgendered. First, ensure the person knows that using your correct pronouns is not optional and that if they refuse, you will take further action to ensure your identity is respected. If they continue to intentionally misgender you, file a complaint with the school, office, or establishment where the offense took place. While they may not be able to make the person use your proper pronouns, they may be able to enforce punitive action and/or limit your interactions with this person.

While it’s best to first come out to people you believe will be supportive, there’s unfortunately a possibility that some people you come out to might react negatively. Sometimes the person may simply need time to adjust and come to accept your identity in the future. Others may have false beliefs about LGBTQ people that may require deconstructing. You might ask them why they feel upset about you sharing your identity with them. Whatever the reason, know your identity is valid and that you deserve acceptance. While it may be difficult for some people to accept, you should not have to compromise your identity for anyone.

Sometimes the simplest explanation is the best explanation. If the person you’re coming out to has very little understanding of the LGBTQ+ community, you may choose to state the way you feel in the simplest terms possible. This may include specifically what your gender or sexuality means to you as well as the actual definition of the label you use to describe it.

If you know another queer person who you feel comfortable speaking to, you might ask them about their own experiences with figuring out their identity. Not all queer people will feel comfortable speaking on their experiences, but many will be willing to share, especially if they know you’re questioning and are seeking guidance. Additionally, reading about or watching videos on other queer people’s experiences may help you with your own journey.

The best way to interact with the queer community anonymously is online. There are queer spaces on nearly every popular social media platform that will allow you to interact with other members of the community without outing yourself. There may also be LGBTQ+ events in your area that take specific precautions for individuals who may not be out yet. If you’re interested in going to an LGBTQ+ event but are concerned about being outed, contact the event organizer to ask how you can safely attend.

The LGBTQ+ community is very broad and, thanks to the internet, finding other queer people has never been easier. One way to meet other queer people is through LGBTQ+ Centers or local LGBTQ+ organizations. There are also lots of LGBTQ+ communities online where you can connect to other queer people worldwide. If you want to meet other queer people in person, however, MyCenter Link is a great resource to help you find an LGBTQ+ center near you that may be able to help.
